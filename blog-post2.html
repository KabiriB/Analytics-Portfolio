Data Visualisation in 2025: Are We Just Making Pretty Pictures?

Published on February 15, 2025

Introduction: The Aesthetic Trap of Modern Data Visualisation

The field of data visualisation has never been more dynamic. With the rise of AI-powered analytics, real-time dashboards, and interactive storytelling, organisations can now present insights more compellingly than ever.

Yet, the uncomfortable question remains: are we actually making better decisions, or are we just making data look good? As businesses, governments, and media outlets double down on sophisticated charts and visual analytics, it is crucial to ask whether we are truly enhancing decision-making or simply engaging in aesthetic-driven storytelling.

The Evolution of Data Visualisation: From Clarity to Complexity

Data visualisation has long served as a bridge between raw numbers and human comprehension. From Florence Nightingale’s polar area charts to Edward Tufte’s principles of graphical excellence, the goal has always been to simplify complexity and guide decisions.

However, today’s visualisation landscape prioritises flashiness over clarity, fuelled by trends like:

  • Hyper-interactivity: Dashboards that offer endless drilldowns, often at the expense of usability.
  • Excessive colour schemes: Eye-catching but frequently misleading, creating cognitive overload.
  • AI-generated visualisations: Often automated without sufficient human interpretation, potentially amplifying data biases.

A prime example is the financial sector, where intricate real-time stock market dashboards flood traders with an overload of flashing indicators. Instead of guiding smarter investment decisions, these dashboards can sometimes fuel reactionary decision-making driven by visualised volatility rather than long-term strategy.

The Dark Side of Data Visualisation: Manipulation and Misinterpretation

Not all visualisations are created equal. While well-intended, data visualisations are increasingly used as tools of persuasion rather than tools of insight. Consider the following tactics:

  • Cherry-picked data points: Selective inclusion of favourable trends while omitting contradictory information.
  • Misleading scales and baselines: Truncated Y-axes can exaggerate changes, making minor fluctuations appear dramatic.
  • Overuse of infographics: Designed for virality rather than accuracy, oversimplifying critical issues.

A well-documented case is the early COVID-19 pandemic data, where varying scale choices in different national dashboards led to drastically different interpretations of risk. Some minimised threats through logarithmic scales, while others amplified panic through exaggerated bar charts.

The Decision-Making Dilemma: Are Dashboards Helping or Hurting?

Dashboards have become ubiquitous, from executive boardrooms to personal fitness trackers. But as we rely more on dashboards to track performance metrics, there is a growing risk of dashboard fatigue— the phenomenon where decision-makers become overwhelmed by excessive data points and conflicting signals.

A 2023 McKinsey report found that over 60% of executives** feel burdened rather than empowered by their organisation’s data dashboards, citing issues like:

  • Overcomplicated layouts that require excessive interpretation.
  • Redundant metrics that obscure rather than clarify key trends.
  • Lack of actionable insights, leaving leaders with more questions than answers.

What Good Data Visualisation Should Do

How do we move beyond “pretty pictures” and ensure that visualisations genuinely support decision-making? Here are some guiding principles:

  1. Prioritise Clarity Over Complexity
    • Simple charts, well-labelled, often communicate better than overly interactive dashboards.
    • Avoid unnecessary 3D effects, excessive filters, and distractions.
  2. Incorporate Uncertainty Transparently
    • Display confidence intervals, error margins, and trend volatility rather than single-point estimates.
    • Use Bayesian models to visualise probability distributions rather than binary predictions.
  3. Use Visualisations to Guide Action, Not Just Inform
    • Every dashboard should answer: What decision does this enable?
    • Highlight cause-effect relationships instead of isolated statistics.

Conclusion: Toward Purpose-Driven Visualization

Data visualisation is not the enemy. When done right, it is one of the most powerful tools available to decision-makers. However, as the industry moves toward aesthetic obsession and AI-driven automation, we must ensure that our focus remains on insight rather than illusion.

In 2025 and beyond, the challenge will not be producing more dashboards or making them more interactive—it will be ensuring that they cut through the noise and guide meaningful decisions.
